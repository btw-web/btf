How to chose the best bike repair stand for your needs

The most important features to look for in a bike stand are certainly stability, versatility, sturdiness, load capacity and, in case you want to bring it along for races and events, its portability and weight. Ah, and there’s the price of course.

Stability first, because you don’t want your bike to trip over you. You want to be able to hold it from different parts without having to worry too much. In general, tripod bike stands are more stable than those with only two legs.

Versatility is the ability to use it for different kinds of work, the possibility of rotating the bike, even 360-degrees or holding it in vertical or upside-down. For “versatility” we also mean the chance to work on different kind of bikes.

Sturdiness, well… we probably want a bike work stand that doesn’t break too easily.

Load capacity refers to the maximum weight that the stand can hold. If you only ride ultralight race bikes you don’t have to worry about it. But if you have different rigs, full-suspension MTB, touring tanks, or even a tandem or an e-bike, then you should consider buying a stand that will hold all of these.

Portability and weight are self-explanatory: being able to perform adjustment before a race or event is great, especially for cycling teams.

If you’re an amateur mechanic who wants a stand for basic repairs and maintenance you should consider also budget models, you probably don’t need an expensive stand for pros.

If you have no space to set up a permanent workshop, consider a portable model, like the Feedback Sports Pro Elite or the Park Tool Team Issue.

Euro-Style Bike Stands vs Clamp Stands – Carbon frame anyone?

Clamp-style stands hold the bike using an adjustable clamp where you can attach the bicycle. Most common holding points are the seat-post, seat tube, and all frame tubes in general tube.

Clamps are usually rotating, most even 360, which lets you put your bike in a position that’s most convenient for what you’ll be working on.

Clamps though can easily damage carbon or lightweight aluminum tubes if tightened too hard – scratches on the paint can be a common problem. They also aren’t great on aero or other non-circular tubes, since the clamping mechanism is usually circular.

Euro-style stands don’t use clamping jaws and instead fix the bike at the axle, front and rear dropouts, or/and bottom bracket. This style is a bit more complicated than a simple clamp, but it makes sense for riders with high-end road bikes, especially if with oddly-shaped tubes.

Axle/bottom bracket mounting stands like the Prepstand X eliminates the possibility of damaging the frame.

A few downsides: If your bike has thru-axles, make sure the stand comes with the appropriate adaptor; To work on front brake and headset you have to flip the bike around and mount it by the rear dropouts; The Euro-style system doesn’t work on long bikes, the length is limited by the horizontal mounting bar.
