Best Bike Touring and Bikepacking Handlebars – Everything You Need to Know

Read Time:12 Minute, 47 Second

Comfort on a bike tour if one of the more important things to take into consideration. And the main body contact points need to have stability when contacting the bike. Accommodating and helping to keep you comfortable through the many miles you will be travelling. The main two areas for bike touring is your saddle and handlebars. In this article we are going to pick apart what to look for in a set of handlebars for touring. And what are your best bike touring handlebars to choose from. Hopefully you will come to a conclusion with enough knowledge to go out a buy yourself some amazing handlebars.

It seems like it is becoming a habit for me to change my handlebars every time I go on a new bike touring adventure. After trying many different types I have some favourites, but I am always up to try some new bars.

My main two favourites are the butterfly bar or trekking bar, and maybe some flared dropbars.

It is a personal preference at the end of the day, so I encourage you to give many different handlebars a go. Because you just never know which ones will work best for you.

For this article I asked many bike tourers and bikepackers what handlebars they personally use and would recommend to a friend. I compiled all my results and will share them with you later on.

But first lets have a look at all the main aspects to consider before you go out and buy yourself some handlebars for your touring bike.

Wrist comfort is a major one. This goes for any kind of comfort on the bike. You are going to be spending countless days, months or even years riding your bike when touring. The handlebars need to relieve any pressure on your joints. This is what makes the best touring handlebars great is they give you the ability to switch around your hand position.

Butterfly handlebars are my favourite for multiple hand positions. You move your hands about in 5 or more different positions, stretching out your arms, and moving them around to prevent them from getting sore, being in the same position for too long. If you have other generic handlebars like a flat bar your hand has this constant pressure in one spot and can cause nerve pain or even damage.

Your handlebar choice can effect your overall bike fit and posture. I have toured with some all-road handlebars before and I found myself leaning too far forward in a more aero, race position. After about 20 days touring this really started to make my back hurt. However, I just recently got some flared dropbars that have hoods that stand higher, and they all ready feel as though they have improved my posture.

Depending on the style of handlebar, will dictate the type of mounting space you will have for gadgets. I found the butterfly bars and 500 mm flared drop bars and many others in this list provide ample amounts of space to mount a, cycle computer, phone, bell and many other accessories to your handlebar. Resulting in a cockpit that looks like that of a spaceship.

Handlebar bag compatibility is something else to consider when choosing handlebars. Make sure to find the length of the bar or check the dimensions of your handlebar bag to see if they will work in unison. Some bikepacking handlebar packs are made for dropbars and could be to long for narrow dropbars. Also If you are looking to get a Rando bag for example you will need to double check.

Different handlebar sizes, and stem clamp compatibility. Different bars come in varying stem clamp sizes. 25.4 mm is standard which os for most MTB handlebars, the butterfly bars I got where this size. And then 31.8 mm which is (OS or oversize) in diameter. However, this size is becoming more and more popular due to the properties of strength and stiffness, the diameter provides.

All Road / Drop Handlebars

Referring to the standard dropbar handlebars you have on road bikes. They are more for the experienced rider. I personally don’t recommend them for touring compared to the flared dropbar option. They can be too narrow making them harder for beginners to manage.

Pros to standard road dropbars

  • Narrow for riding through traffic
  • Quicker and more responsive cornering, helps manoeuvring quickly around a hazard.
  • Provide multiple hand positions, drops, hoods, back and front
  • Allowing some straight back posture on the flats
  • Able to get down in the aero position, to battle head winds and be more efficient

Cons to standard road dropbars

  • Don’t provide as much comfort on rough terrain
  • Not the very long bars, making it harder to fit bikepacking handlebar packs/rolls with enough space for hands.
  • More difficult for less experienced riders
  • Result in bad posture for extended trips, causing a sore back
  • Harder to access break levers

I am going to be honest and not recommend any specific handlebar options for these type of handlebars. As they are just not the best option out there for touring bars. There are so much better bars in this list. Although, having that said, if they are the only bars you have. Especially the ones that came with your traditional bar-end shifter touring bike. They work just fine, I am more referring to the handlebars that are from road bikes as these are very narrow.

Flat / Riser Handlebars

flat are common on some expedition touring bikes they are my second last preferred type of touring handlebars in this list. But don’t get me wrong they are still good, depending on your preferences. Some tourers recommend, MTB Riser Handlebars with Ergon Grips.

Pros to Flat / Riser Bars

  • You are able to gain better control with flat bars compared to most
  • When riding out of the saddle they give you leverage
  • Ease of swapping grips
  • Many mounting options for accessories
  • A good choice for the beginner bike tourer

Cons to Flat / Riser Bars

  • Unless you use bar-ends like Ergon Grips, these bars only provide really one main hand position, which is mainly why I don’t recommend them as much as others.
  • Depending on the handlebars width they can be difficult to navigate through tight traffic.
  • On standard flat handlebars they are not ergonomic for your wrists

Flared Dropbars

These type of handlebars are my favourite at the moment. Recently getting some Funn G Wide 500 mm Bars fitted on my Surly Long Haul Trucker. Flared dropbars offer some great stability and extra cushioning when riding the rough stuff.

They have found popularity in the cycle-cross and gravel racing communities where control over off-road surfaces is important. But for touring they offer a range of hand positions, reducing fatigue.

All the extra bar length means heaps of potential for mounting whatever your heart desires. So much space allowing you to also fit handlebar bags with ease. Flared drops feel more natural than conventional drops.

Somethings to keep in mind with flare drops is to clamp them up fairly tight and also they make it harder to lean your bike against a wall.
