Marin Bikes – Ultimate Bike Brand Guide for Bikepacking & Touring

Read Time:14 Minute, 18 Second

Marin bikes offer great value for the money! Most of their bikes are really budget friendly and rather good options for those looking to get into bikepacking and/or bicycle touring. I am starting a new blog series what should I call it? I wad thinking something like Bike Brand Guides! But not just for any type of cycling of course the bikes I choose will be related to this wonderful world of adventure cycling. So the idea is to take a bike brand and talk about all their bikes that are great for bikepacking and bicycle touring. And of course todays brand is Marin Bikes.

So before we get into the different types of bikes over at Marin. Lets cover some questions that you might be wondering.

Are Marin Bikes Good?

Marin makes a wide range of models, from exotic, full suspension, high tech rippers, to bike path cruisers. They’re committed to cycling’s wide range of disciplines. If you’re a hard-core mountain bike rider, or a casual cyclist, there is something for you. There brand ethos is that bikes make us better people: healthier, more relaxed, and happier. But are these bikes really any good? Well what do I mean? You know build quality, and all that! How well do they stand up to the demands of cycling. For those of us looking for an adventure bike we need something that is going to stand up to demanding conditions.

Compared to bigger brands like Giant and Cannondale, Marin is lesser known but does this mean their bikes are just as good as these big brands? They are probably not the best quality that you could buy. But you are also not forking over large amounts of money for a bike. They make the ultimate budget friendly bikes. With most of their bikes there is a lot that could be upgraded, parts wise. Especially the cheaper options. Overall the brand is okay. It is best to compare bikes at a individual level for accurate determination of what is ‘good’. My best advice is to compared bike models and read reviews for more information.

Where Are Marin Bikes Made?

From Marin County, California and established in 1986. The Marin brand was born in a cycling mecca, and they ride the same trails and routes that bred mountain bikes and many of the most prolific and innovative brands in our sport. At 30+ years old, they continue to have the same childlike fascination and obsession with bikes that brought them to this industry.

They specialise in mountain bikes but also offers several other variants. Many of its bike models are named after locations in and around Marin County. But they do have quiet a few options for bikepacking and bicycle touring.

Marin Bikes For Bikepacking & Touring

All right now that we have got the full list of Marin bikes available online out of the way. Time for all their bikes that are great for bikepacking, touring and any other type of adventure riding! In no particular order here are their options. 

Marin Four Corners

So the Marin Four Corners is one of the better choices of Marin bikes for that bike adventurer. Who dream of heading off the beaten path and cap outdoors for days at a time. While the type of terrain which this entails is pretty much covered on this bike. Weather it be pavement, dirt road or light single track.

The Four Corners is available in either the new 2020 ‘standard’ and Elite models. this sees both bike options in a full steel frame build, with 650b, 700c wheel size, and no differences in geometry. The main differences is the Four Corners Elite features, SRAM Apex 1 Hydraulic Disc brakes while the standard has Tektro Spyre-C Road Mechanical Disc brakes. The main difference being the groupset and gearing. The Elite offers mid-range component levels with a APEX 1 build with a 1X11. The rear cassette sees 9-44T and a FSA Gossamer Pro, Megatooth 38T Chainring. While the standard model is more of a modest component level featuring the Shimano Sora 9-Speed groupset with a 3×9. That means a Shimano Sora Hollowtech II, 50/39/30T at the front and Shimano HG300 9-Speed, 11-34T rear cassette. 

One of the things that stood out to me was that the Elite only has one eyelet either side of the front fork. And the Marin Four Corners has 2 on each fork. Not a big deal but slightly less mounting options on the elite. Making it harder to fit front cargo cages. 

The Marin Four Corners costs $1,099.99 at Jenson USA and the Marin Four Corners Elite is $1,999

Marin Headlands

Are you after a carbon fibre gravel bike? The Headlands 1 & 2 have taken teachings from Marins Gestalt X line of aluminum gravel bikes and incorporated that knowledge into the Headlands. Marin have incorporated long, low, slack, geometry numbers that have taken over mountain biking. This results in a engaging and exciting ride no matter the riding conditions. This bike features the 1X gravel drivetrain which Marin have shorten the chainstays to 420mm while maintaining tire clearance for 700x45c tires. 

So what are the main differences between the Marin Headlands 1 & 2 models? The only differences is in the groupsets. The Headlands 1 features a mid-range SRAM Apex 1×11-Speed, with a Forged Alloy 1×11, Hollow CrMo Spindle, Narrow-Wide 40T crank. And SunRace 11-Speed XD, 10-42T cassette at the back. While the Headlands 2 has a high-end component level with Shimano GRX 800. That means still a SunRace 11-Speed XD, 10-42T (same as the Headlands 1). But it has a FSA Gossamer Pro front crank, Megatooth 42T Chainring. Both have Hydraulic Disc brakes but different types. Also the Headlands 2 has a dropper seat post when the Headlands 1 has a rigid. 

For the higher spec’d Headlands 2 you are looking at $2,649.99 and the Marin Headlands 1 comes in at $2,249.99. Both at Jenson USA. 

Marin Gestalt X

The Gestalt X is the mountain bikers drop bar bike, featuring the all reliable and simple 1x drivetrain. The reason for Marin to create the Gestalt X was to allow riders to expand their horizons beyond the all-to-familiar paved road. A few things that they though about when creating this bike was adding generous tire clearance, lowering the stand over, using solely one-by drivetrains, adding dropper post routing, and tuning the geometry for more adventurous riding.

Marin Nicasio

The Nicasio comes in four different bike options. You get the choice between the Nicasio Ridge, Nicasio 2, Nicasio + or the Nicasio. This bike was originally made for the similar purpose as the Gestalt bikes. The Nicasio and plus models feature Marins Series 1 frames, giving you everything you need to tackle roads, gravel, and trails. While the Ridge and Nicasio 2 models have the Series 2 frames to up the game with lighter, snappier tubing and premium features like thru-axles, flat mount brakes, and tapered head tubes for full carbon forks.

These line of bikes especially the Marin Nicasio + offers some really great value for how cheap it is. You get a Cromoly frame and fork that provides unique ride characteristics same as the Standard Nicasio as well. And both these models include mecanical disc brakes and the plus rolls on 650b x 47c tires, while the standard option has 700 x 30c tires. You only get a cheap 1×9 setup on the Nicasio + and Shimano Claris 2×8 groupset for the standard Nicasio. 

When taking a look at the next two more premium models (Nicasio 2 and Nicasio Ridge), they both feature a Marin Full Carbon fork that has Taper Steerer, 12mm Thru-Axle, Flat-Mount Disc. My favourite pick out of these bikes would be the Nicasio Ridge with the SRAM Rival 1 upper mid-range components, I also have a thing for the gravel 1X setups and it looks good on this bike! The bikes comes with WTB Byway, 650Bx47 tires and the Nicasio 2 comes with WTB, Riddler Comp, 700Cx37 tires. The Nicasio 2 is a mid-range component level with the Shimano Tiagra 2×10. 

Marin Lombard

The Marin Lombard is the type of bike better for city commuting and light touring. I say light as it considered a touring bike on some sites. But personally I am not sure I would test it on some really epic expeditions around the world. I am sure it might do okay but not willing to say it would. Having that said, it is still a more then capable commuter and if you plan to do touring with minimal gear this bike is worth considering. But also it is a good gravel bike too. 

The Lambord 1 is really ideal and shines when used for urban riding. The bike features reflective decals to increase your visibility, and the durable components and high-volume tires (700 x 40c) allow you to treat the city like a playground. The bike has a aluminium frame and fork. Tektro Road Mechanical disc brakes offer consistent and powerful braking performance. Festuring a modest Shimano Sora 2×9-Speed groupset with a Shimano HG-200, 11-34T rear cassette and Shimano FC-M210-2, 46/30T crank at the front. 

While the Lambard 2 offers some better upgrades and overall better bike. Replacing the alloy forks with some more supple Marin Full Carbon fork. With a slightly improved Shimano Tiagra 2×10 setup with FSA Omega MexaExo, 50/34T crank and Shimano HG-500 Tiagra, 10-Speed, 11/34T cassette and hydraulic disc brakes. The bikes is rolling on some Vee Tire G-Sport, 700x40c tires. 

Marin Pine Mountain

The last bike we are going to look a bit further into is the Pine Mountain this is one of the many MTB bikes that Marin offers. When I am sure you could use some of their other bikes for bikepacking and adventure trail riding. This bike is specifically designed for just that. When comparing these bikes I noticed that thy are quiet similar even when they have different gearing. For a cheaper price, if you opt for the Pine Mountain 1 compared to the 2 you are still getting a decent bike when it comes to things like the upper mid-range component level specs. Which is about the same range as the Pine Mountain 2. But still having that said the Pine Mountain 2 is just more capable. It has some more upgrades like a dropper seatpost and slightly different front forks.

But the biggest difference is in the groupset and drivetrain. The 1 has Shimano Deore with 1×11, Marin Forged Alloy 32T crank and SunRace 11-Speed, 11-46T cassette. When the 2 has Shimano SLX 1×12, FSA Grid 32T crank and Shimano SLX M7100 Cassette, with 10-51T.
