Kona Rove Vs Kona libre – 2021 Models Compared

Read Time:12 Minute, 9 Second

The Kona Rove and Libre battle! Well which bike is best? We are going to compare these rather popular drop bar gravel bikes side by side and see what the difference is between each. In this article we are going to compare 6 different models together. Rove AL 650 vs Rove DL vs Rove LTD vs Libre AL vs Libre CR vs Libre CR/DL

So what’s the difference? The Kona Rove is considered an adventure ready drop bar bike. While the Libre is considered by Kona as a drop bar bike for big gravel, big climbs and big adventures. I would go further to say that the Rove is more a commuter gravel bike around town or is more suited to someone looking for an entry level bike into the gravel scene. The Libre is more suited to the rider who is looking for all out performance and speed on gravel roads and potentially races.

The new 2021 models have some options and we will touch on which is best for the different types of intended uses. When it comes to price the Rove is cheaper starting at $949 up to $2,499, while the Libre starts at $1,799 up to $3,799.

The line up in more detail!

2021 Kona Rove AL 650

The 2021 Kona Rove AL 650 is their entry level model for their entry level gravel bike in the Rove. This means it is the cheapest bike out of all bikes compared here. Also available in the 700c AL option, a perfect entry-level bike suitable for pavement and tire clearance big enough for off-road riding.

In comparison to the two other Rove’s (which both have identical geometries) this Alloy option has some slight variations. With a slightly longer stack, trail and top tube length. And also a slightly shorter front centre, wheelbase, rake and stand over height. Also each bike varies from components and parts, see the specs comparison table for reference.

This Alloy Rove comes with a 2 × 8 Claris and FSA drivetrain which is considered their modest end component level. You still get the 650b wheels with WTB Venture Comp 47c. Mechanical disc brakes and the drivetrain is made up of FSA Tempo Adventure, 34/50t crank and Shimano Claris 11-34t 8 speed cassette. Which is not the best by any means at climbing out of all the bikes here with a 26.54″ climbing gear which is actually slightly better then the Libre AL and CR/DL which is surprising. And a highest gear of 120.74″ for the downhills, that means a 1 to 4.55 gear ratio. That downhill gear on this Alloy Rove is the best on all the bikes here.

2021 Kona Rove DL

The 2021 Kona Rove DL formerly called the Rove ST, features a stunning Kona Butted Cromoly frame and Project Two Cromoly Disc fork. It is the steel gravel bike option out of the lineup, if steel is your thing. The mid priced bike option out of the Roves.

Both the 2021 Rove bikes have the exact same geometry but this Rove has a full steel build with the Kona Project Two Cromoly Disc fork. And is powered by the 1 x 11 Rival 1 upper mid-range groupset. And has mechanical disc brakes instead of hydraulic. Breaking down the groupset, the bike has a SRAM S350, 40t X-Sync crank and SRAM PG1130 11-42t cassette. Still with 650b wheels you do get the same WTB Venture TCS DUAL 650x47c tires and WTB KOM Light Team i23 TCS 2.0 wheels.

You do get a more limited climbing gear at 25.21″ while the highest is 96.59″ giving you a gear ratio of 0.95 to 3.64

2021 Kona Rove LTD

The 2021 Kona Rove LTD is Kona’s new and improved Rove, back better than ever. A steel frame gravel bike that includes high end components (tied with the Libre range) with a versatile 2 x 11 gravel groupset and the Rove Verso Full Carbon flat mount disc fork, with hydraulic disc brakes. A Steel frame and carbon fork combination is hard to beat for adventure riding comfort.

Gearing! It features a 2 x 11 GRX 800, GRX 600 (high-end) groupset with the Shimano GRX 810, 31/48t crank and Shimano 105 11-34t cassette. Rolling on some 650b WTB Venture TCS DUAL 47c tires. Giving the bike a 24.15″ climbing gear and a 115.69″ highest gear. That is a gear ratio of 0.91 to 4.36.

There is a few other differences when it comes to parts between all these bikes as you can see below the more descriptive sections of each bike, I have included a table comparing all the differences for a more visual guide. Also we will touch on the differences in geometry later on.

2021 Kona Libre AL

Now onto the Libre models, first up we have the entry level Libre which is built from a Kona 6061 Aluminum Butted frame and paired with a Kona Verso Full Carbon Flat Mount Disc fork which is the same fork used for the Rove LTD and the other two higher-end Libre models. Because you get an allot frame this makes this bike the most affordable out of the Libre lineup.

Rolling on some WTB Riddler TCS 700x45c tires, this bike is specced out with mid-range components and mechanical disc brakes. Featuring a 1×11 Apex setup. This means a SRAM Apex, 40t X-Sync crank and SRAM PG1130 11-42t 11 speed cassette. Resulting in a 26.63″ climbing gear and 102.03″ highest gear for the downhills. Giving the bike an overall 0.95 to 3.64 gear ratio.

2021 Kona Libre CR

If you are after an all carbon build for the cheapest price, this Libre CR is your go to. Plus it comes with the best climbing capabilities compared to all the other options here. With 23.35 climbing gear inches. With high-end components and hydraulic disc brakes. The bike has a 2×11 GRX 800, GRX 600 groupset. With a Shimano GRX 600, 30/46t crank and Shimano 105 11-34t 11 speed cassette.

2021 Kona Libre CR/DL

Last is the Libre CR/DL, which is another all carbon build like the CR but with better parts. However it is a little limited at climbing with a 26.63″ climbing gear, thanks to the 1×11 GRX 800 groupset, with Shimano GRX 810, 40t crank and Shimano SLX 11-42t 11 speed cassette.

Almost everything on this bike is premium! You even get a TranzX Dropper Post +RAD Internal 31.6mm. Along with some other upgrades like; Easton EC70AX handlebars instead of just Kona Road bars, Easton EA 70AX Wheelset, and Shimano GRX 810 hydraulic disc brakes.

Geometry – Rove Vs Libre

Geometry can play a big part in the difference of how I bike rides and feels. It is definitely something to consider when buying a bike. All the measurements below are in mm and are for 54 cm sized bikes.

So as you can see from the table above, both the DL and LTD Rove models have the exact same geometry and slightly different for the AL. If we start by comparing the Rove’s with the Libre (which is rather similar across the board with some slight variations. We will see that the Rove has a little bit more aggressive or less relaxed gravel bike geometry. But still on a rather comfortable side compared to more racer geometry gravel bikes out there. This is evident with the 435 mm and 1048 mm wheelbase on the Rove. Compared to the 440 mm and 1058 mm wheelbase on all the Libre models. The shorter chainstay on the Rove makes the rear of the bike feel like an all-rounder with a bit of quickness. Which is perfect for lightweight bikepacking! While all the Libre models have a 440 mm chainstay that puts them in the mid to long category for gravel bikes making it feel rather chill in the rear.

Across all the bikes there is a disparity when it comes to trail lengths. The Rove DL, LTD and Libre CR all have the same 61mm trail for a 54cm bike. Which is worth noting that these lengths change even further as you go for different sized bikes. And it is non liner. However, for the example give here. This 61mm trail on these bikes makes them feel rather predictable to control up the front and is in the mid length for similar gravel bikes. Then the Rove AL, Libre AL and CR/DL all have longer trails. With the Alloy Rove at 66mm and the other Libre options at 67mm it puts these bikes in the high trail length category making them feel stable but not sluggish.

Both the Rove and Libre have similar front centres with 5mm difference. The Libre at 628mm giving you a little more room for gear up front without the worry of hitting your toes.

The stack/reach ratio on the Rove is lower then the Libre making it less upright, but it is still above 1.5 which is consider upright enough for long days in the saddle and maintain comfort. But the Libre have a 1.58 putting them rather upright, compared to the Rove’s 1.52.

The standover height for each bike again varies and is not linear to each model. If you are considering either bike I would highly recommend to suss out each bikes geometry in your desired frame size because again they vary.

Overall it depends on what you plan to do with the bike, to decide ultimately which is best. It is such a hard choice as both have great features. Both bike models are rather limited with only up to 47 mm tires. At least that is the max Kona recommends.

Ultimately the Rove is better suited to those beginners in gravel riding and are just looking for a cost effective entry level bike to crush some gravel. While the Libre is that carbon gravel machine you go to when you want speed or a more effective gravel bike.

Each individual bike is different again even when it comes to gearing. But being able to climb up steep sections on single track or forest roads is a bonus and having the lowest gearing inch possible is a must in my opinion. Which is why I like the Libre CR, it is the cheapest all carbon gravel bike from Kona. The Libre seems slightly more capable when it comes to strapping gear to your bike and going on rad adventures, which is what we are all about here at Cycle Travel Overload! Thanks to the longer wheelbase and other geometry specs like more upright riding position for extra on bike comfort. But if you are on a budget then you still can’t go wrong with the Rove.
