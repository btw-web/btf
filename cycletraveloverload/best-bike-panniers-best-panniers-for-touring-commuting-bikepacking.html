What to look for in bike panniers?

Like I mentioned before there are many attributes to consider when looking at some bike panniers for your rig.

Waterproof

One of the more common factors when looking at some panniers is whether they are waterproof or not. Some people will disregard any pannier that is not waterproof, full stop. This used to be me, but as I become more open the the world of all the different pannier options out there, I see an interest in others. There are some highly water resistant options, that still do almost just a good a job. If you are really concerned you may want to put your most valuable gear in waterproof bags.

We will talk about what you intend to use the panniers for later, but for me I mostly use them for 1 to 3 month long bike touring trips. And rain and wet conditions are expected. So for me personally I prefer fully waterproof panniers. As I would prefer to have some dry clothes at days end. It really does depend on where you will be travelling and the time of year.

If you are looking for a great waterproof pannier option that give you a decent amount of space for storage I highly recommend the Ortlieb Classic Panniers which are 20 litres each. And bomb proof. I have been using them for a couple years now and they have exceeded all my expectations. They have kept all my gear bone dry on really wet tours.

Non waterproof options seem more popular with commuters and serve a purpose here. As most times you are going to ride when the weather is best and it is not a concern. Most handmade pannier options are non waterproof and offer some custom designs and add ons that you may not get with general panniers. Offering additional, storage options on the pannier.

Bag Volume

The volume of your panniers really depends on your intended use, how you will be using them, for commuting to long year plus expeditions. How long you plan to be travelling for will indicate whether you have just two panniers or a fully 4 pannier setup. With smaller Ortlieb City type panniers at the from and larger Ortlieb Classics at the rear. Maybe you intend to do short weekend or week long trip you may only need some small Ortlieb Gravel panniers and some other bike bags for your setup.

Learning to live with less does help. Personally I have been on 3 month long bike tours with just two rear panniers, and not requiring the use of a front set. That is even with all my camera gear and extras for filming YouTube videos along the way.

If you are planning a bikepacking trip. But still want some larger capacity bags, (compared to standard bikepacking seat post packs). So you can store a laptop and other gear that may not fit in standard bikepacking bags.

There is a growing niche of ‘micro, nano or mini’ panniers. Which are smaller style panniers made specifically for bikepacking. That maybe a better option for you. Designed with the cutting of weight in mind. Something like the Revelate Nano Panniers are smaller bags that strap over the back of a rear rack.

Generally, the smaller the bags and panniers you take the less stuff you take. As humans we tend to want to fill the space that we have. Similar to that idea of eating with a smaller plate makes it feel as though you eat more. But I always go for the bigger plate.

Pannier Hardware

Depending on the type of panniers you choose, the hardware can vary. If you opt for a Ortlieb panniers you get a hard plastic build on the back side of the pannier, with complete adjustability to the hooks. Allowing them to move across to fit best on your rack. Also plastic adjustable hooks that attach to the rack preventing the pannier from bouncing of the rack on rough terrain.

I was surprised to hear that some people a weary of such design as they believe the plastic could snap. But from my own experience using my Ortliebs they have had no such problems. They only thing is the rubber inner hook stabilisers do fall out if you are not noticing them when taking the panniers on and off. Which are easily replaceable.

The other option with handmade pannier designs. Is the old school, bungee hook style. Where there is two more permanent metal hooks that attach to the top of the rack. Followed down by a adjustable bungee cord that runs down to another hook that attaches further down on the rack. However, these may seem obsolete in design, they actually work very well.

Another option is made by Arkel, the Cam-Lock Hook Kit. Designed for upgrading your existing panniers if the hooks are worn or broken. A little more complicated to use. But another option if you want to figure it out for yourself.

Pros to using panniers

  • Cary heaps of gear, more space for food.
  • Very easy to remove from racks and toss in the tent, very easy to find gear in the suitcase-style openings.
  • Also quick to remove
  • You don’t have to go with less gear and have to compromise and cutout comfort.
  • No need to go out and spend on ultralight and compressible gear. Panniers could essentially save you money on having to spend on all that ultralight gear intended for bikepacking.
  • They are more suited for longer trips allocating all that extra space for whatever you need to survive for months and months.
  • Panniers give you a lot of versatility
  • Having panniers front and back means you can pack almost EVERYTHING youâ€™re concerned about needing.

Cons to using panniers

  • As you may not to be as discipline when packing panniers compared to bikepacking bags. Panniers make the bike wider and may be more likely to snag and bounce around making an annoying rattle on some more rough terrain. While Bikepacking bags are more streamlined and manoeuvrable, but they also enforce a little more packing discipline then panniers.
  • Don’t get me wrong you can still ride with panniers on off-road conditions. But, they are not ideal on gravel or trails.
  • It feels when riding that panniers create a fair amount of drag. And may not be as aero as the likes of bikepacking bags.
  • It is heavy, if you prefer to ride faster, panniers will slow you down a little. Affecting the way the bike feels and preventing a agile bike feel
  • With the added weight, it can be harder to hike a bike if you choose to go off-road with panniers and you are fully loaded. Same goes with just riding up hills, you need to make sure your bike is capable of riding up hills with a full load with its current gearing setup.

What is your intended use for panniers?

So how are you planning on using panniers? Are you just going to be using them for the daily commute? A week long bikepacking trip? A three month long bike touring adventure or multiple year bike touring expedition? Your intended time frame will mean the more gear. If you are riding for more then 6 months you may need to pack gear for summer and winter meaning you may need four panniers.

The more space you have on the bike to pack the more comfort and more easy going the packing is. Actually, I take that back I am not sure how easy going it would be to pack this bike above and also remember where everything is.

But on the other hand the less space for packing, means more decisions and discipline. A lot more thinking needs to go into how you will store all the need gear for your trip.

Fitting panniers to your bike – Different types of mounting systems

When looking for a mounting system for your setup. You need to consider; where are you going, what weight are you expected to run? The most common mounting setup is a rear rack and/or front rack, allowing the panniers to fix on the bike.

Ideally a rear rack should have two points of attachment. Two arms that attach to the seat stay under the saddle is ideal. Try and avoid the ones that only have one arm.

Also rack build and material is another consideration. Ideally you want a racking system that is made from steel. Some racks are made from soft aluminium and various mounting systems leave deep marks on it. Potentially compromising strength in the long run.

The importance of a good touring rack is huge, you don’t want to get one and have it break on you. It is also important to make sure the rack you get is compatible with the panniers. Or else your panniers may bounce off on rough roads.

When mounting racks on your bike it is also important to use the right sized mounting screws. If you do however find that your screws do get loose. A great product that helps lock and seal threaded fasteners is blue Loctite.
