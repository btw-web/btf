Ultimate List Of Bikepacking Gravel Bikes – Gravel Bike For Bikepacking

Read Time:14 Minute, 56 Second

Is a gravel bike ideal for bikepacking? In my opinion just about any bike can be okay for bikepacking. Even the bike you have. But that doesn’t mean it is ideal.

Bikepacking can mean different things to different folks. The definition for me is essentially strapping bags to a bike and going camping, with long miles in between.

Some gravel bikes are very capable bikepacking bikes. And some aren’t so much. So what makes a gravel bike a good bikepacking bike?

It really comes down to your personal preference and where you sit on the comfort vs performance. Do you like to be super comfortable on bike? But also want the best performance possible with comfort? Like me?

Geometry is were gravel bikes can vary. Some are designed to be more cyclocross orientated, for racing and others can super sluggish and boarder-line touring bike. It seems finding the happy medium is where you can find that optimal gravel bike for bikepacking.

The bike for me has to have an upright riding position. This is one of the more important criteria. Some gravel bikes have a more road bike inspired geometry meaning lower stack/reach ratios. Ideally you want a gravel bike to have a 1.5 stack/reach ratio or higher for a comfortable upright riding position.

A gravel race bike will typically have a shorter chainstay and wheelbase. While a gravel bike for bikepacking will have a slightly longer chainstay and overall wheelbase.

Along with difference in geometry gravel bikes best suited for bikepacking often have larger tire clearance. Although you can still use a road bike for bikepacking. Which goes back to my point that you can use just about any bike for bikepacking.

But if you plan to explore on single track and off to roads less roamed. Being able to run tires past 45c is superb. Ideally a gravel bike that is opted well for bikepacking will allow for up to 2.3″ tires and even some 650b wheel can get 2.5″ tires.

It seems racer type gravel bikes run 38 to 40 or even 42c tires. While adventure ready bikepacking gravel bikes come stock with 50mm or 2″ to 2.2″ as an industry standard.

Gravel bikes for bikepacking will also have stronger wheels. Again gravel bikes for racing have around 24 spokes wheels while rigs designed for carrying gear will have around 32 spokes. Resulting in a stronger wheel.

The flared drop handlebars on bikepacking gravel bikes typically come with more degree flare. From 16° to 24° flare, while race gravel bikes may have less, at around 14°. It all depends on different bike builds. But this more flare in the drops makes for more comfort and arm stretch on long rides.

Gravel bikes by nature tend to have underperforming climbing gears. They are not the best for climbing steep hills while the bike is loaded. They seem to be more orientated for speed on the flats and downhill, with all-road conditions.

But if you choose wisely you can find relatively good climbing gears on some 1x and 2x gravel setups. The better climbing gravel bikes that I have come across tend to have about 22″ climbing gear which is still not 18″ MTB territory.

This is what makes MTB’s a more ideal bikepacking bike. You can slowly slog up hills with an easy climbing gear even on a loaded bike.

The Ideal Bikepacking Bike

Unfortunately a “gravel bike” is not really considered the ideal bikepacking bike. If you want a rig that is dedicated to bikepacking you may want to look into hardtail mountain bikes. Some make the point that the flat bar offers better mounting options and it is part true that the flat bar offers nice and upright riding comfort.

But don’t get me wrong you can find some gravel bikes that have all of the above criteria to make it more bikepacking capable.

I am personally a big fan of steel gravel bikes, this material is strong and forgiving when put through the test on bikepacking adventures.

On a budget? Want to find a bikepacking gravel bike but don’t want to spend all your hard earned coin? Bikes like the 2021 Poseidon Redwood are the perfect option!

Can you ride a gravel bike on singletrack?

It depends on your skill level and the type of gravel bike. I would not recommend riding a gravel bike with 45 mm or slimmer tires. Even 50mm with tube setup you can expect punctures, especially on more gnarly singletrack sections.

It is very possible to ride on easier singletrack. If you want a challenge riding gravel bikes on singletrack can be fun! But again it is more ideal to ride a gravel bike, that has clearance for 2.2 or 2.3″ MTB tires. Which helps tremendously with absorbing singletrack vibrations.

If you want to explore mostly steep, rocky, single-track terrains while bikepacking then a hardtail or full-suspension MTB is your best bet. However if you plan to ride a 33/33/33 mix of road, gravel and some single-track, a “well equiped” gravel bike is a plausible option! Meaning a gravel bike with all the above criteria.
