Complete List of 40 Best Bikepacking Tents

Read Time:20 Minute, 8 Second

When choosing a tent you need to ask yourself some questions. As the tent you should really get should come down to your personal preference and needs, depending on your type of adventure.

How long are you travelling for? Are you going on a multi year trip? Where you need to take a long lasting quality tent. Which cost a bit more money? Or are you just looking for something cheap? Just to get you through a couple of weeks of bike adventuring?

Are you a couple who need quite a lot of living space? Or a solo rider travelling by yourself? It is often important to note that is you are a slo traveller looking for the best tent for solo bike touring. It maybe a good Idea to opt for a 2 person tent. As a 1 person tent can often be too small for you and all your gear you wish to cart into your tent. And if you are exploring for multiple months or more. A 1 person tent can get very small after sometime.

Things to consider for a bicycle travellers tent?

  • Weight
  • Pitch instantly
  • Size (1 person, 2 person or 3 person)
  • Stay up in heavy winds
  • Suited for all climates
  • Privacy
  • Space to live
  • Room for all of your luggage
  • Even vestibules or gear shed
  • Possibility for taking in amazing views
  • Keep you dry from wet weather
  • Dry out quickly
  • Can pack wet
  • Last for along time
  • Pitch anywhere
  • Number of entrances
  • Ventilation
  • Dry pitch
  • Stealthiness and Colour
  • Ground sheet
  • Packed Size

What types of tents are suited for bikepacking?

When looking at a tent for bikepacking the weight is one of the more important factors. You need something that is going to be light enough, it is becoming easier and easier now days with companies making tent suited for this ultralight style of travel. All the tent in this article below are all light enough to suit a bikepacking setup for your next adventure.

The packed volume of the tent is another thing that makes a tent suitable for bikepacking. Depending on the bikepacking bags you have. Generally a tent is stored in the front handlebar bag on a normal bikepacking setup. Getting a tent that fits in these dry-bags with extra room to store other gear is ideal.

Some of the tents I was going to include in this have been discontinued by the manufacturer, for example, the Big Agnes Happy Hooligan UL 2 and some others. So instead I decided not to include those ones in this complete list of bikepacking tents.

So without anymore of me going on to much about bikepacking tents, lets get into the best bikepacking tent options. So I asked a hundred or so bikepackers what tent they use for their bikepacking adventures. This is what they all use.

Not sure about bikepacking with a tent? Maybe you want to cut more weight? Another option is a tarp setup, you can use your bike as a bike tarp camping method. These are generally a lighter option however they have drawbacks.

Tarp vs tent

I have considered camping with a tarp compared to the normal tent. I like the idea of having a very minimal camping gear setup. With less weight to carry on the trails.

Here are some pros and cons, when it comes to the difference between the two.

Tent pros

  • Be set up pretty much anywhere
  • Durability
  • You can pretty much find any tent suited to your style of bicycle travelling such as the ones mentioned above.
  • Tents are installation, keeping warm air in during cold nights.
  • Separation from the environment, keeping bugs out.
  • Privacy to change clothing and to have your own closed and private space.

Tent cons

  • Pitching a tent can be difficult at times if you’re pitching in the dark or battling with the elements
  • During warmer temperatures, heat can get trapped in the tent and you can wake up in a sweat.
  • Some tents especially 4-season tents can add a considerable amount of weight.

It must be said that I really enjoy camping under the stars and this is why I want to try tarp camping. My only concern is staying drug when it pours down rain.

Tarp pros

  • Provide a open camping experience if you really enjoy the wilderness
  • Can also be useful if you need some shade during hot rides where there is not much shade to take a break.
  • Provides plenty of ventilation to keep your cool on warmer nights
  • They are lightweight. You don’t need to carry as much compared to a heavier tent.

Although the idea of camping with a tarp some of the cons are something to think about before making the leap.

Tarp cons

  • Exposed to unwanted bugs and critters.
  • Due to the design there may be water that gets you wet depending on the angle of the rain.
  • Due to the airflow with a tarp, it will get cold during colder months. Less of a windbreak compared to a tent.
