Buyer’s Guide

When buying a new backpacking hammock, there are so many factors to consider. From weight, to dimensions, to strength, there is a lot that goes into your research before finding the perfect one.

We’ve luckily written this guide to help you out and highlight what you should be keeping an eye out for.

Weight And Packability

If you plan on traveling far and wide with your hammock in your backpack, you’re going to want something lightweight and compact. It needs to fit in snugly and not weigh you down on the trail.

Luckily, a lot of hammocks that are designed for backpacking trips are now easily compressible, but ensure you read up on this feature first. You’ll also need to take into account any accessories that come with it as these can take up extra room and add extra weight.

Accessories include tarps, straps, or nets and though they are needed with some hammocks, sometimes they fit into the little compressible bag so it might be worth researching this too if you’re a smaller camper or have a relatively small backpack.

Dimensions

You’re going to need a hammock that fits your body. This will provide ultimate comfort and therefore, the best night’s sleep. You need to note down how tall you are and then add a little room on either end so that you don’t feel restricted.

Our top tip would be to buy a double hammock even if it’s only one person that intends to sleep in the hammock. A double hammock offers much more room for optimum comfort.

The width however of your hammock is down to personal preference. If you like to wrap up at night, buy a wider design so you can almost turn yourself into a cocoon.

Strength

After a long day hiking, the last thing you want is your hammock breaking. This is why you want a hammock that is strong enough to hold your weight and durable.

You need to know how much you weigh and compare it against the maximum weight capacity of your chosen hammock. Overloading can not only cause the material to weaken and break but reduce its overall lifespan.

Having a strong hammock doesn’t always have to break the bank either. Just ensure you find something with high-quality, long-lasting materials that have been tested and reviewed.  

Suspension System

This is what helps to hold you up at night. A suspension system on most hammocks consists of straps and carabiners yet some hammocks don’t have a proper suspension system at all.

If you want to be flexible with where you set up your hammock, however, make sure you opt for a hammock with longer straps. This means you’ll have more options with your attachment points and can stretch them enough to reach generously spaced-out trees.

Moreover, if you want to opt for something that doesn’t require too many tricky knots, go for a daisy chain or carabiner setup but if you want to build your own, you’ll need whoopie slings and suspension systems. 

Accessories

Most hammocks come equipped with a mosquito net, straps, and a rain fly and if you want a weather-resistant hammock, this rain fly and a rain tarp are vital.

Some hammocks are even waterproof themselves so keep an eye out for this as when that sudden downpour appears, the last thing you want is to end up being drenched right through.

Purpose

The final thing you may want to consider is the purpose of your hammock.

Yes, since this is an article on the best backpacking hammock, you’re going to want one that is suitable for backpacking trips but the difference between a day trip or a thousand-mile-long adventure is significant.

The purpose will affect how much you want to spend on your hammock, how heavy you’re willing to carry, or how many extra accessories you are going to need.

Additionally, if you plan to use your hammock for sleeping in instead of a simple shelter when it’s warm out or to relax around camp, you’re going to need one that’s a lot more durable, weather-resistant, and insect-proof, so consider this before spending your money on something that isn’t suited for your individual situation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What do you need to go hammock camping?

You can create your own hammock camping setup by adding individual components to your current hammock.
However, like some of the kits we have recommended above, a prepackaged “hammock tent” is your easiest option and saves you worrying about if you have remembered everything.
If you are going to create your own, however, you need to remember to pack a hammock, a suspension system, an underquilt or sleeping pad, a rain tarp, and bug netting.

Is a hammock better than a tent?

In certain situations, hammocks can be better than tents. For example, if you want lightweight, portable camping equipment for the ultimate backpacking adventure, a hammock is easier to carry around than a whole tent.
Moreover, to sleep in, they provide better back support than sleeping on the ground in a tent. However, bear in mind that tents are good in situations where you need a little more privacy, higher safety standards, and higher insulation value.

How cold is too cold for hammock camping?

As a general rule, hammock campers go for a tent at around 32 degrees.
If you want to camp in a hammock in below zero temperatures, you need to do your research and go properly prepared with all the right equipment. You may even need to withdraw if conditions become too unsafe.
