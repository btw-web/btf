Buyers Guide

Looking for a 3 Season sleeping bag that will be comfortable in warmer weather around 32F / 0C?

Environment

When purchasing a winter sleeping bag for cold temperatures, it’s important to carefully consider when and where do you intend to use it. Taking into account the weather conditions and the environment in which you will take your trip is imperative to finding the right one for you.

Camping in winter can be quite a challenge, with cool or freezing temperatures, strong winds, maybe heavy rain, or a huge dump of snow possible. Ensure that you choose a sleeping bag that will keep you warm no matter what the conditions you’re in.

Shape

The two most common that can be found are mummy-shaped and rectangular. Mummy-shaped bags are designed with heat retention in mind, whilst being smaller and more convenient to carry on your bag as you enjoy your trip.

They follow the contours of the body to keep you wrapped up warmly, which, whilst being the optimum shape for heat retention, does mean that there is less room for maneuver.

The hood on these ensure that you can stay warm in the harshest of climates, whilst those with a drawstring help to protect your face from the elements.

Rectangular bags are much wider from head to toes, allowing a more relaxed fit, with more wiggle room for the user. Incredibly comfortable and suitable for those who are bigger or taller. But not suited for cold weather camping.

They can often be bulkier to account for the additional material, as such, they can weigh more, which can be an issue if you’re engaging in a long or challenging trip.

The hood on these can vary, though often they include a half-circle mummy-style hood, which increases the comfort and protection from the elements.

Temperature Rating

Most manufacturers will explicitly state the temperature range and suitability of their products. This makes it easier to select the most appropriate sleeping bag for your needs. Often they are labeled one, two, three, or four seasons, but what does this mean?

When buying a winter sleeping bag, you should aim for a four-season product. The Winter sleeping bags in this review are rated to around 0F / -17C or lower. Most of the sleeping bags for winter camping in this review have options for much colder models if needed. If you suffer from cold feet, you could consider a pair of Down Booties to keep your feet warm.

These bags are designed with extreme temperatures in mind, with durable, water-resistant outer shells and a down or synthetic-down filler.

Lining Fabrics

The lining fabric plays a big part in both the retention of heat and the comfort of the user. Often they are lined with nylon, polyester, or taffeta, which are soft to the touch, breathable and smooth, ensuring comfort when nestling down for the night.

Sleeping bags for use in more extreme climates all use a lightweight, strong lining fabric.

Shell Fabrics

Nylon, polyester, and taffeta are also used for the outer shell of sleeping bags. Although these are comfortable and breathable though they might struggle in wet conditions.

Many companies have developed their own textiles, such as DryLoft, which are designed to be hydrophobic and breathable whilst still keeping you warm and dry.

Ripstop is a reinforced fabric that is widely used, it’s still light enough to remain breathable whilst increasing the durability and therefore lifespan of the sleeping bag.

Pertex seems to be the best standard for outer face fabrics on most cold expedition sleeping bags.

Filling

Synthetic – Synthetic insulation is the most widely used form of insulation in sleeping bags. Often a supplier will use a mixture of fabrics and manufacturing techniques to aim to provide suitable insulation even in the more challenging environments.

Some of these fabrics can be on the heavier side, which may affect your choice if you’re embarking on a bigger adventure.

Down – This is the plumage underneath the outer layer of feathers on birds, usually in sleeping bags goose or duck down is used. These keep the birds warm in cold weather, therefore they are perfect for ensuring a warm night’s sleep.

This insulation strikes a balance between being incredibly warm as well as lightweight, with a better warmth-to-weight ratio than synthetic filling.

More expensive sleeping bags will usually use down or a synthetic-down combination to ensure the highest level of warmth and heat retention. These bags are more easily compressed for convenient storage and travel.

All the best Winter Sleeping Bags use high fill power down.

Zips

Sleeping bags have either one or two-sided zips, whilst some may also unzip at the bottom near the feet. Zips can be a source of cold air sneaking into the sleeping bag, however, most quality manufacturers ensure that they include draft tubes to prevent this from happening.

Look out for products that contain anti-snag zips, as these ensure that you won’t accidentally damage the inner lining or outer shell of the sleeping bag. Even the best quality winter sleeping bags will sometimes snag the zips when opening and closing.

One-sided zips may well retain heat more efficiently, though two-sided zips allow for greater ventilation and faster drying when hanging up. Zips at the bottom or feet allow ventilation and breathability if you are overheating at night.

Water Resistance

When camping in winter, it is essential to take into account the possible extremes of weather. High-quality sleeping bags will often be labeled as DWR, or, durable water-repellent. This can save you, your belongings, and your sleeping bag from becoming damp and difficult to dry.

After extensive use, the DWR coating may well wear out, and you might notice that water is seeping into the bag, or perhaps it’s taking longer to dry. You can buy a specifically designed spray to reinforce the water-resistance of the bag.

Some down filler is also treated to be hydrophobic, to increase water resistance and allow faster drying, which can be essential when you’re outside braving the elements.

Draft Tube

This is an insulated tube that keeps warm air in and prevents the cold air from entering the sleeping bag along the length of the zipper. These are essential when camping in colder or more extreme climates and environments.

Baffles

Either horizontal or vertical, baffles keep the filler insulation distributed evenly throughout the sleeping bag. They greatly aid in the retention of heat and stop any cold spots from forming.

Shoulder baffles are designed to seal the bag around your shoulders and neck, ensuring that there is maximum heat retention.

Pockets

Many sleeping bags include pockets, external, internal, or a combination of the two. These are incredibly convenient as a safe space to store any valuables or essential amenities. The sizes can vary greatly, so it’s important to ensure that they will be a suitable size for your needs.

External pockets will include a zip for added security, whereas internal pockets will usually be sealed with Velcro for easy access.

Footbox

Sleeping bags with a foot box are designed to provide more room and maneuverability for your feet. Instead of adjusting your sleeping position, you’re able to sleep with your feet in a more natural position, providing comfort and limiting any possible circulation issues.

Compression Sack

These are designed for convenient storage and travel, removing the need to meticulously roll up the sleeping bag and attempt to fit it into the bag.

With these, the sleeping bags can be stuffed inside and held in place with a drawstring. Often they have straps to allow easy attachment to your backpack or camping bag.

How Do I Know The Temperature Rating Of My Sleeping Bag?

Most manufacturers will explicitly state the temperature rating of their product. They will often provide a wide temperature range in which the user will still maintain comfort and warmth.

What Is The Warmest Sleeping Bag Lining?

A down or down/synthetic combination will provide you with the warmest sleeping bag. Using the birds’ plumage and natural defence against the cold to retain heat and provide comfort even in harsh environments.
